Calling Filipinos “Monkeys” Is Racist, Not Clever

The insult fails twice: Filipinos are human, and monkeys are intelligent, social animals. The racism is the only embarrassing part.

Every so often, a racist discovers the word “monkey” and presents it as though they have just completed a doctorate in zoology.

No evidence. No argument. Just an animal comparison, a few laughing emojis, and the expectation of applause.

The intended message is not subtle. Calling Filipinos “monkeys” is meant to portray an entire people as primitive, unintelligent, uncivilized, or less human. The insult depends on two false assumptions: that saying it can reduce Filipino humanity, and that monkeys are mindless creatures.

Neither assumption survives contact with reality.

The factual record: Filipinos are human beings. The long-tailed macaque, Macaca fascicularis, is the only monkey species naturally found in the Philippines. Monkeys are capable of complex social recognition, learning, and, in some populations, selective tool use. None of those facts makes the racial slur harmless. They simply reveal that it is both scientifically foolish and morally dehumanizing.

The science is already ruining the insult

Animal intelligence is not measured by whether a species can pass a human examination, operate a smartphone, or spend an evening losing an argument in a comment section. Intelligence develops around the problems an animal must solve in its own environment.

Macaques live in complicated social worlds. Studies of rhesus macaques show that they can connect the face and voice of a familiar individual, evidence that they maintain mental representations of socially important companions. That research concerns rhesus macaques, not Philippine long-tailed macaques, so it should not be stretched into a claim that every monkey species behaves identically. It does, however, make one point very difficult to deny: macaques are not socially oblivious animals.

Researchers have also documented Burmese long-tailed macaques in Thailand using stones to open shellfish and nuts. In field experiments, those macaques selected stones of different masses depending on the food they were processing. Smaller stones were associated with attached oysters, while heavier stones were selected for larger or more resistant foods.

That is task-sensitive tool selection. Or, in language more familiar to the internet, quality control.

Not every long-tailed macaque population uses stone tools. A separate study of captive, inexperienced long-tailed macaques found that they did not spontaneously learn the same pounding behavior merely because stones and food were available. That limitation matters. Good science does not turn one impressive observation into a magical power assigned to an entire species.

Still, the evidence is more than enough to retire the lazy idea that “monkey” automatically means stupid.

Some macaques recognize familiar individuals across sight and sound. Some long-tailed macaques select tools suited to a task. Meanwhile, some people have access to the accumulated knowledge of humanity and use it to post racial slurs.

Nature has a sense of humor.

What monkey is native to the Philippines?

The answer is the long-tailed macaque, Macaca fascicularis. A peer-reviewed study describes it as the only monkey species found naturally in the Philippines. The same species also occurs across much of Southeast Asia, including countries such as Thailand, Malaysia, Indonesia, Vietnam, and Cambodia.

This requires a taxonomic correction because “the monkey species found only in the Philippines” is an attractive headline, but not an accurate one.

The National Center for Biotechnology Information lists the Philippine long-tailed macaque as the subspecies Macaca fascicularis philippinensis. Its own taxonomy page warns that the database is not an authoritative final word on classification. The American Society of Mammalogists’ Mammal Diversity Database treats Macaca fascicularis as one broadly distributed species that includes the Philippines.

The careful wording is therefore this: the Philippines has a native population commonly called the Philippine long-tailed macaque, and some taxonomic systems recognize a Philippine subspecies. It is not universally accepted as a separate monkey species found nowhere else.

Also, the Philippine tarsier is a primate, but it is not a monkey. “Primate” is the larger category. Monkeys, apes, humans, and tarsiers occupy different branches within it.

The racist intended “monkey” to mean inferior. Science returned adaptability, memory, social awareness, and problem-solving.

So what exactly were you trying to say about Filipinos?

Adaptable?

That is not much of an attack. Filipinos build lives across regions, languages, industries, and countries. We create community in places that may initially feel unfamiliar and turn distance into connection.

Social?

Again, not devastating. A Filipino can meet another Filipino abroad and, within one conversation, discover a shared province, school, employer, relative, friend, or person who somehow knows someone’s auntie.

Resourceful?

You may have accidentally described a national survival skill. Filipino households have extended the useful lives of appliances, furniture, recipes, businesses, and family budgets through improvisation that would make a design laboratory nervous.

Expressive?

We sing at celebrations, reunions, campaign events, quiet evenings, and gatherings that were not originally advertised as concerts.

None of this means Filipinos are biologically comparable to monkeys. We are not. It means the insult is so poorly constructed that its intended contempt keeps collapsing into qualities people normally respect.

It is less a racial argument than an accidental performance review.

Why calling Filipinos “monkeys” is still racist

This is where the satire stops being enough.

Calling a racial, ethnic, or national group an animal is not an innocent lesson in zoology. It is animalization: a form of dehumanizing language used to push people outside the circle of equal dignity and moral concern.

The United Nations has repeatedly warned about language that describes groups as animals or pests. Its educational material on the path from words to violence identifies dehumanization as a process that strips people of humanity and moral consideration. In June 2026, the UN Secretary-General again described hate speech as a step down the path of dehumanization.

That does not mean every ugly comment automatically leads to mass violence. It means societies should understand the mechanism. Repetition makes contempt feel ordinary. Animal labels make humiliation easier. Once an audience is trained to see a group as less human, discrimination becomes easier to excuse.

Monkeys being intelligent does not make the slur acceptable. It makes the stereotype scientifically wrong while the racist intent remains morally obvious.

The scientific correction says: the animal you chose is smarter than your insult assumes.

The moral correction says: Filipino humanity never depended on your permission.

Satire should challenge power, not erase humanity

Satire can be sharp, rude, exaggerated, and uncomfortable. It can expose hypocrisy and ridicule abusive power. But placing an entire people inside an animal caricature is not automatically clever because somebody added music, animation, or a punchline.

FMB&CO. News previously examined this distinction in its analysis of an AI-generated video that portrayed Filipinos through monkey imagery. The issue was not that political disagreement had occurred. The issue was that nationality itself became the object of animalization and humiliation.

A serious political argument should be able to criticize governments, alliances, policies, and legal positions without reducing millions of ordinary people to a racial image.

If the argument disappears when the slur is removed, there was never much of an argument.

Francine’s perspective · Commentary

Filipino dignity is not a debate prompt

Filipinos do not have to submit a list of achievements every time someone questions our worth. We do not earn humanity by being talented, hospitable, resilient, educated, successful, or useful to another country.

We are human before the résumé, before the passport, before the applause, and before anybody decides whether they respect us.

We can answer racism with facts and humor without begging the racist to approve of us. The purpose of the reply is not to audition for dignity. It is to name the tactic, expose its ignorance, and refuse the frame.

The monkey was never the embarrassing part

Monkeys did not invent racial hierarchies.

They did not colonize countries and then describe the people they exploited as uncivilized.

They do not create anonymous accounts to demand evidence that Filipinos deserve ordinary human respect.

They respond to food, danger, territory, relationships, and survival. Some humans respond to insecurity by searching for a nationality to insult.

So the next time someone calls Filipinos “monkeys,” there is no need to accept the comparison. Correct it.

Filipinos are human. Monkeys are intelligent animals. Animalizing a people is racism, not wit.

The monkey was never the embarrassing part of the sentence.

The racism was.

Quick answers

Are monkeys intelligent?

Yes. Research documents social recognition in macaques, and some long-tailed macaque populations select and use stone tools suited to different foods. These abilities vary by species, population, and context.

What monkey is native to the Philippines?

The long-tailed macaque, Macaca fascicularis, is the only monkey species naturally found in the Philippines. Some databases recognize a Philippine subspecies called Macaca fascicularis philippinensis, while broader taxonomic treatments differ.

Is the Philippine tarsier a monkey?

No. The Philippine tarsier is a primate, but it belongs to a different branch of the primate family tree and is not a monkey.

Why is calling Filipinos monkeys racist?

The phrase is used to animalize Filipinos and deny equal human dignity. Calling racial or national groups animals is a recognized form of dehumanizing rhetoric, regardless of the intelligence of the animal being invoked.
