Zambales · In memoriam

Remembering Amor Deloso, Former Governor of Zambales

A public life that crossed generations, political eras and repeated returns to service in the province he called home.

Verification note: FMB&CO. News received a report that former Zambales governor Amor D. Deloso has passed away. At the time this private draft was prepared, an official family or government announcement confirming the exact date, place and cause of death had not been independently located. Those details are deliberately omitted and must be verified before publication.

For decades, Amor D. Deloso was not merely a name on a ballot in Zambales. He was part of the province’s political memory.

His public career stretched from the municipal government of Botolan to repeated terms at the provincial capitol. It survived changes of national administration, shifting local alliances, defeats, comebacks and the constant argument over what development should look like in a province shaped by mountains, farms, mines, coastlines and the West Philippine Sea.

Following reports of his passing, Zambaleños are left not only with grief, but with the difficult and necessary work of remembering a long public life honestly.

From Botolan to the provincial capitol

The public record places Deloso in elected local service as early as 1971, when he won the mayoralty of Botolan. A 1989 Supreme Court decision later recorded that he had been elected governor of Zambales in the January 1988 local elections.

He would become one of the province’s most persistent political figures, returning to the governorship in later periods and winning another comeback in 2016. The Philippine News Agency described the 2019 contest between Deloso and Hermogenes Ebdane Jr. as a clash of political heavyweights. That description captured more than one election. It reflected a province in which leadership had become intertwined with rival networks, family names and competing ideas of continuity.

Deloso’s political life was defined by return. He could lose office and remain present. He could be written off and reappear in another contest. Whether one supported him or opposed him, few could describe him as a temporary figure.

Some leaders occupy an office. Others become part of how a province remembers time.

Environment as a political line

Among the clearest themes of Deloso’s later governorship was his public position on mining. Upon returning to office in 2016, he issued Executive Order No. 1 temporarily stopping mining operations in Zambales. The order came amid legal and community challenges concerning environmental damage in Santa Cruz and nearby areas.

The Department of Environment and Natural Resources later cited the provincial executive order alongside complaints, environmental proceedings and a Supreme Court writ when it suspended mining firms operating in the province.

This did not settle every dispute over mining, rehabilitation, livelihoods or enforcement. It did, however, place the provincial government publicly on one side of a question that continues to shape Zambales: who benefits from the extraction of its natural resources, and who carries the damage after the wealth leaves?

The coast, the fishermen and Bajo de Masinloc

Deloso’s record was also tied to the fishing communities of Zambales. In 2018, during a dialogue involving fishermen, military officials and government agencies, he promised provincial support for alternative livelihoods for those affected by tensions around Scarborough Shoal, or Bajo de Masinloc.

He argued that fishing was too important to the province to be treated as an afterthought. The public discussion at the time involved reports of Filipino fishermen losing portions of their catch and asking for greater protection and access.

For coastal towns, these were not distant geopolitical abstractions. They were questions of food, income, shelter during storms and the right of ordinary people to continue a livelihood inherited across generations.

Governance beyond the campaign stage

Official DILG records from his 2016 administration show Deloso participating in the formulation of an Executive-Legislative Agenda and a capacity development plan with provincial officials, national agencies and civil society representatives. Other government records place him at the head of provincial peace-and-order and anti-drug councils.

His administration also used the annual Dinamulag Mango Festival to promote agriculture, tourism and provincial identity. In 2019, he described Zambales as more than a mining province and presented it as an emerging tourism destination.

These records matter because political memory often shrinks leaders into campaign colors and rival surnames. Government, however, also happens in workshops, disaster meetings, budget priorities, consultations, ordinances and the slow coordination that rarely produces a dramatic photograph.

A long career is never remembered in only one way

No honest memorial should turn public service into mythology.

Deloso’s career unfolded inside the intensity of Zambales politics. It included legal battles, hard campaigns, public criticism, alliances that changed and decisions that remain open to judgment. Longevity does not place a leader beyond scrutiny. Death does not require a community to erase disagreement.

Respect and accountability are not opposites. A province can mourn a person, acknowledge decades of service and still preserve a complete public record. In fact, that is the more dignified form of remembrance.

Some Zambaleños will remember projects and assistance. Others will remember political contests, disappointments or personal encounters. Families may remember a different man from the official recorded in government documents. All of those memories can exist at once.

Francine’s reflection · Zambaleña perspective

A province must learn how to keep its memory

In Zambales, too much of our history survives only because someone still remembers a name, a story, a place or a decision. When that person is gone, part of the record can disappear with them.

That is why the passing of a public figure should be more than a day of photographs and condolences. It should remind us to preserve speeches, executive orders, local newspapers, government documents, oral histories and the stories of ordinary people who experienced an administration directly.

Amor Deloso belonged to a political generation that shaped the province across several eras. We do not honor that history by making it perfect. We honor it by recording it carefully, discussing it honestly and allowing future Zambaleños to understand the choices that built the province they inherited.

The measure left to Zambales

Public office ends. Public consequence does not.

The roads, policies, institutions, environmental disputes, political alliances and community memories connected to a leader continue long after the final campaign. They become part of the landscape through which the next generation must govern.

Amor Deloso’s place in Zambales history will not be determined by one tribute. It will be shaped by official records, by the people who worked with him, by those who challenged him and by communities that lived with the results of his decisions.

For now, the province pauses for a man whose public life crossed generations.

May his family and loved ones be given privacy, strength and peace. May Zambales remember him with compassion, accuracy and the courage to preserve its own history.
