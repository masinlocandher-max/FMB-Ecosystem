Pax Silica’s Water Question Is Real. The “Almost All of Luzon” Claim Is Not.

The proposed New Clark City hub has no published project-level water budget. A speculative model points to a serious local capacity test, but it does not prove the project would consume nearly all of Luzon’s supply.

The claim that Pax Silica could use “almost all of the whole Luzon water supply” is not supported by the available evidence. It takes a legitimate concern, the heavy water demand of semiconductor manufacturing, data centers, and mineral processing, then stretches it beyond what any public project plan currently shows.

The stronger story is less dramatic and more important. The proposed hub could become a major new industrial water user in Central Luzon, while the government has not yet published a project-level water budget, final tenant list, source allocation, recycling target, or drought plan.

FMB&CO. verdict: Unsupported. No single official figure called “the whole Luzon water supply” appears in the reviewed sources. No final Pax Silica facility mix or official water-demand forecast has been released. A high-demand scenario is useful for stress testing, but it is not a confirmed project.

What has actually been announced

The Philippines and the United States announced a planned 4,000-acre, or roughly 1,618-hectare, industrial hub in New Clark City after the Philippines joined the U.S.-led Pax Silica initiative in April. The project is intended to support artificial intelligence infrastructure, semiconductors, advanced manufacturing, and critical-mineral processing.

Reuters reported at the time that details were not final. Specific companies and facilities were still to be settled. That distinction matters because water demand depends on what is built, how large it is, what cooling and manufacturing systems are used, how much water is recycled, and where the water comes from.

Where the 151 to 303 MLD estimate comes from

An author-uploaded April paper on ResearchGate constructed a hypothetical “full-scale” zone with three semiconductor fabrication plants, two hyperscale data centers, and one mineral-processing plant. It estimated combined demand at 151 to 303 million liters per day, or MLD.

That is a scenario, not an official Pax Silica forecast. The paper identifies itself as independent research and ResearchGate does not present it as a peer-reviewed project study. Its imagined facility mix has not been announced by BCDA, the Philippine government, the U.S. government, or named private tenants.

20 to 30 MLD

Current New Clark City requirement

BCDA figures reported in February described the city’s present water requirement at this range.

150 to 200 MLD

Expected citywide requirement

Reports on the proposed New Clark City water project placed full-development demand in this range.

151 to 303 MLD

Independent hypothetical scenario

This estimate assumes a facility mix that has not been confirmed as the Pax Silica plan.

The comparison still reveals a serious local question. The hypothetical low end is close to the expected water requirement of all New Clark City at full development. The high end is above the reported 150 to 200 MLD range. If a future tenant mix resembles that scenario, water infrastructure, source security, treatment, and reuse would need to be addressed before operation.

Why “almost all of Luzon” fails the scale test

The Philippine Statistics Authority recorded 93.08 billion cubic meters of freshwater withdrawals nationwide in 2024. Averaged across the year, that is about 255,000 MLD. The speculative 303 MLD high end is roughly 0.12 percent of that national figure, not anything close to all of it.

That is not a perfect local comparison. National withdrawals include different uses and do not tell us what is available in a particular Central Luzon watershed during a dry month. It does, however, make the order of magnitude clear. The “almost all of Luzon” wording is not credible.

A false continental-sized claim can distract from the real local question: who gets water first when supply becomes tight?

What the government should publish before approval

  • A facility-by-facility annual and peak-day water budget, not only a zone-wide estimate.
  • The proposed water sources, existing permit allocations, seasonal reliability, and aquifer or watershed impacts.
  • Separate targets for potable water, industrial water, recycled water, and discharged wastewater.
  • Required technologies for closed-loop reuse, dry or hybrid cooling, leak control, and ultrapure-water recovery.
  • Drought triggers that reduce industrial use before households, hospitals, and food production are harmed.
  • Independent monitoring with results available to local governments, farmers, communities, and the public.

BCDA has already been working with K-Water and Maynilad on a proposed New Clark City water and wastewater system. That planning is relevant, but a citywide system proposal is not a substitute for a specific Pax Silica water account.

Francine’s perspective · Opinion

I want the investment. I also want the water terms on the table before the ribbon cutting.

The Philippines should not be afraid of advanced industry. We should be prepared enough to negotiate it well. Good jobs, stronger skills, and higher-value manufacturing can be worth pursuing. None of those goals requires us to be vague about water.

Families and farmers should never discover the true cost of a technology zone only when pressure drops from the tap or irrigation becomes uncertain. Publish the numbers early. Require recycling. Set drought rules. Make the companies prove efficiency before scarcity asks ordinary people to adjust.

Responsible ambition is still ambition. It simply knows that a modern economy cannot be built by treating a basic human need as an invisible input.
